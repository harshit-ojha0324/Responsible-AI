The Core Legal Challenge

The "Black Box" Liability

AI regulations (GDPR Art. 22, EU AI Act) increasingly demand "meaningful information about the logic involved."

The Gap: Outcome-based supervision (just checking the final answer) hides the reasoning process.

The Risk: A model might reach a "correct" decision based on illegal, biased, or hallucinated logic. This creates hidden liability.

Research Question

"Does forcing an AI to 'show its work' (Process Supervision) actually make it safer and more legally auditable than just checking the result?"

Reliability

Can we trust the decision?

Faithfulness

Does it follow valid logic?

Auditability

Is the cost of review low?

Study Design: Simulating Audits

The Proxy: Complex Reasoning

We used the GSM8K dataset as a testbed. While mathematical, it requires multi-step logic similar to legal reasoning chains.

Three Compliance Conditions

  1. Outcome (Black Box): "Just give the verdict." (Final Answer only)
  2. Process (Explainable): "Explain your reasoning." (Chain-of-Thought)
  3. Structured (Auditable): "Format your reasoning for review." (Step-by-Step)

Case Study: "Right for the Wrong Reasons"

Outcome Model: "The Answer is $16."

Process Model (Hypothetical Error):
1. 3 + 5 = 10 items (Error).
2. 10 * $1.6 = $16.
Final Answer: $16

The Compliance Risk

Both models give the "Correct" answer ($16). The Outcome model passes audit.

But the Process model reveals a Hallucination in Step 1. In a legal context (e.g., sentencing guidelines), this logic would be grounds for appeal.

Takeaway: Process supervision enables Due Diligence.

Implications for AI Governance

GDPR Article 22

Process supervision technically satisfies the "Right to Explanation" by exposing the logic path.

EU AI Act

High-risk systems must be transparent. Our data shows structured prompts reduce audit costs significantly.

Trust & Liability

Outcome-only systems are liability traps. Process supervision shifts liability from the "black box" to verifiable steps.

Limitations & Future Work

Limitations

  • Domain Transfer: We used math (objective) as a proxy. Legal reasoning is more ambiguous and subjective.
  • Sample Size: 50 annotated deep-dives (labor intensive).

Future Work

  • LegalBench: Apply this pipeline to the LegalBench dataset to test on actual statutes.
  • Automated Judges: Use "LLM-as-a-Judge" to scale up the verification of legal reasoning steps.

Conclusion

"Transparency is not just a technical feature. It is the prerequisite for legal accountability."
